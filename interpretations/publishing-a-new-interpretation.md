# Guidelines for publishing a new Interpretation
In order to declare a change in Interpretation, it is necessary to publish a work to base that change upon. A good declaration of a change in Interpretation contains the following:

- A declaration of the geographic extent of examination
- Examination of a reasonable suite of physical specimens from the entire region
- A statement of the types or, if more appropriate, the various Interpretations that are affected
- An explanation of the situation
- A clear, usable, guide to identification that will enable the reader to determine with the same confidence and result as the author

In addition, this declaration of change should be without barrier to access. This includes subscription-only journals.

## Refer to existing literature where possible
It can often be advantageous to refer to existing literature rather than repeating information. For example, if a change of A to B is to be reverted on the basis that the change was never true, then there is little point re-writing the identification guide for 'A'. Instead, the work can simply say list any number of keys which provide the correct result for A, which will also work for the new Interpretation being described.

An Interpretation can, when stripped down to pure functionality, be declared in a *very* succinct manner:

> We have examined all material within Great Britain and found no presence of Y. Therefore, the split of X and Y is invalid. All GB keys which provide a result of X therefore provide the correct result.
